About

Hello! I am Sean Najmi. Chemical Engineer, Scientist and Occasional Writer.

My research looks at the intersection of experimental and theoretical techniques to elucidate the fundamentals of catalytic biomass conversion. A list of presentations and publications can be found in my CV. I am currently co-advised Andrew J. Medford and Carsten Sievers. Learn more about the Medford Group and Sievers Group.

Research Experience

September 2017 - Present

Georgia Tech
Graduate Research Assistant

Funded by the National Science Foundation Graduate Research Fellowship Program (NSF-GRFP). We are currently studying the catalytic features of certain metal oxides to help us better understand the fundamentals behind heterogeneous catalysis when applied to biomass conversion. We employ ultra-high vacuum (UHV) spectroscopic measurements complemented by density functional theory (DFT) calculations. We are collaborating with faculty at other universities and scientists at US national labs.

June 2014 - May 2017

University of Maryland, Baltimore County
Undergraduate Research Assistant

Funded by both the Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency (DARPA) and an undergrad research award grant. My research focused on the immobilization of the glutamine and glucose binding proteins onto the surface of transparent microbeads for use in a biosensor. I used a variety of techniques such as sensor development, protein purification, gel electrophoresis amongst other biochemical engineering assays. I worked in the Center for Advanced Sensor Technology and was advised by Govind Rao and Leah Tolosa.

Summer 2016

Caltech
Undergraduate Research Intern

Funded by Southern California Edison through the WAVE Fellows program. The WAVE Fellows program seeks to make institutions like Caltech more visible to students from traditionally underrepresented groups. I used DFT to study the thermodynamics and kinetics of the carbon dioxide reduction reaction. We worked on an iridium based pincer catalyst and studied the mechanism by which carbon dioxide is reduced to formic acid. I was advised by William A. Goddard III in the Materials Process and Simulation Center.

Summer 2015

University of New Mexico
Undergraduate Research Intern

Funded by the National Science Foundation through an Research Experience for Undergraduates (REU) Program. I worked on the synthesis and characterization of 3D graphene nanosheets as supports for palladium nanoparticles. The end goal was to employ these supports for use in Proton Exchange Membrane (PEM) fuel cells. I was advised by Plamen Atanassov.
